How to choose Japandi furniture

Furniture is where a Japandi room is won or lost. Get the big pieces right and almost anything you layer on top works; get them wrong and no amount of styling rescues it. Three things to check before you buy.

Buy the material, not the look

A honey-oak finish and honey oak are not the same thing. Read the spec, not the photo: "solid oak" or "solid ash" is what you want for legs, frames, and anything that takes weight; a solid top over a plywood back panel is a fair compromise on price. What ages badly is painted MDF and thin veneer over particleboard — the edges chip, the corners swell, and it never earns the patina that makes wood feel calm. Pay once for solid where it counts.

Low, legged, and a little apart

Japandi furniture sits low and stands on visible legs, so the floor reads as open space instead of being boxed in. Choose pieces a size smaller than you think and leave a hand's width of air around each one — the negative space is the design. A low bed, a slim console, a side table you can see under: the room breathes.

What to skip

High-gloss surfaces, ornate hardware, and a third wood tone (see the two-woods rule below). If you rent, prioritise freestanding pieces over anything that needs drilling — most of the lineup here is renter-friendly by design.

Two woods. Never three.

Pick two woods and commit — usually a lighter (oak, ash, white pine) for the larger pieces and a darker (walnut, dark oak) for the accents. Three woods reads as inventory. Two woods reads as a home.

Skip painted-finish furniture. Skip veneer-on-MDF. Solid wood costs more once, then forever stops costing.
