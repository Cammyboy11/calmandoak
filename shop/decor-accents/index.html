How to choose Japandi decor

Decor is where Japandi is easiest to overdo. The skill is subtraction — fewer, better objects with room to breathe.

Negative space is the design

Leave surfaces mostly empty so the few things you keep can actually be seen. One considered object beats a shelf of them — the art of negative space.

One stem, natural materials

A single dried branch in a stoneware vase says more than a full bouquet. Wood, clay, paper, and dried botanicals over anything plastic — the single-stem rule.

Group with intention

When you do cluster objects, use odd numbers and vary the height, and balance the visual weight across the room (the 30-30-30 rule). Skip knick-knacks and matchy decorative sets.

One object reads as intentional.

One object is editorial. Three objects are decoration. Five objects are clutter.

The Japandi rule for surfaces: pick the one piece that earns the spot, and let it sit alone. Negative space around an object is doing as much work as the object itself.

The space between matters as much as the things in it.
