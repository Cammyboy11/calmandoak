The Japandi entryway

The entryway is the first thing you see coming home and the last thing you see leaving — and usually the most neglected. It's also the easiest room to transform, because it's small. Here's the whole thing.

An entryway runs on the same five principles as the rest of the home, compressed into a few square feet. Four moves do almost all of it.

Anchor it with a console

A slim oak console is the entryway's one piece of furniture — narrow enough not to block the path, with a surface for keys and a basket below for shoes. If the space is tight, a floating shelf does the same job. Find consoles and slatted benches in the furniture edit.

Hide the chaos

Entryways collect clutter — shoes, post, bags, leashes. The Japandi answer is to give each a home that disappears: a tall woven basket for shoes, a lidded box for the small stuff, hooks for the bags. This is storage that doesn't look like storage; browse the baskets in the storage edit.

Light the welcome

One warm light changes the whole feeling of arriving home. A pair of plug-in linen sconces flanking a mirror or print, or a small basket lamp on the console — warm 2700K, never an overhead glare. See sconces and table lamps in the lighting edit.

One calm vignette

On the console, keep it to three things: the lamp, a small tray for keys, and one object — a stoneware bowl, a single stem. The empty space around them is the point. One muted accent is plenty.
