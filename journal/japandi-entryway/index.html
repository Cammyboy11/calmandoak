The Japandi entryway

The entryway is the first thing you see coming home and the last thing you see leaving — and usually the most neglected. It's also the easiest room to transform, because it's small. Six moves do almost all of it.

Why this guide: The entryway is the smallest room in most homes and the one that quietly sets the tone for everything else. We've built ours, photographed it, and pressure-tested the proportions against a year of arriving home in winter coats and damp boots. An entryway runs on the same five Japandi principles as the rest of the home, just compressed into a few square feet. That compression is actually a gift — there are fewer variables to get right, so a small, deliberate composition reads as more intentional than the same effort spread across a living room. The six moves below are ordered so that each one earns the next. Get the console right and the lighting follows naturally. Get the lighting right and the vignette becomes obvious.

1. Anchor it with a slim console (or a floating shelf)

The console is the entryway's one piece of furniture, and the single decision that does the most work is its depth. In a tight hallway, anything over 12 inches deep turns the console into an obstacle — you brush it with your sleeve, you bump it with a bag. Aim for 10 to 12 inches in narrow halls and 14 to 16 inches in more generous spaces. The width follows the wall: a console should span roughly two thirds of the wall it sits against, leaving breathing room on either side.

Solid wood — oak, ash, walnut — sits at the centre of Japandi furniture for a reason: it ages quietly, hides the inevitable scuff, and reads as warm even under cool light. Slatted bases or open shelves below the surface keep the visual weight low; this matters in a small space, where a heavy lower box would close the room in. If the floor under the console is the same wood tone, choose a console one shade darker or lighter — matching exactly flattens the composition.

If the entry is genuinely tiny — a flat with the door opening straight into a living space, or a narrow vestibule — a wall-mounted floating shelf at chest height does the same work in half the depth. A 6 to 8 inch deep shelf gives you a surface for keys, a small lamp, and one quiet object. The Japandi entry is a function, not a furniture set. The console we use A slim solid-wood console at roughly 12" deep and 42" wide — narrow profile, tapered legs, the right scale for a hallway that has to handle daily traffic without crowding it.

2. Hide the chaos in baskets

Entryways collect more clutter per square foot than any other room in the house: shoes, post, bags, leashes, umbrellas, the random charger that someone left on the console "for a minute" three weeks ago. The Japandi answer is not to fight the clutter but to give each category a home that disappears into the composition. A tall woven basket — water hyacinth, seagrass, or jute — under or beside the console swallows shoes without announcing them. A lidded box on the shelf holds the small stuff: keys you don't carry daily, sunglasses, the dog's brush.

The hidden rule here is that woven texture reads as quiet even when it's working hard. A wire grid bin holding the same shoes would scream "storage". A water hyacinth basket holding the same shoes reads as a sculptural object that happens to be functional. This is one of the core moves of storage that doesn't look like storage, and the entryway is where it pays off most.

Wall hooks for bags and coats should be wood or aged brass, never plastic or chrome. Five hooks at varied heights is more useful than four hooks in a straight line, because real life has tall coats and short ones. If you can't drill, a slim wall rail with leather loops gets you most of the way there. Browse the baskets and storage pieces in the storage edit.

The shoe basket we use A tall woven storage basket sized to fit roughly six pairs of adult shoes — water-hyacinth weave, generous opening, sits beside or under the console without announcing itself.

3. Light the welcome (warm, never overhead)

The single biggest mistake in most entryways is lighting them from above. An overhead fixture — even a beautiful one — flattens everything it touches and creates what designers privately call "hospital arrival": bright, even, judging. The Japandi answer is side light at warm 2700K colour temperature, never above 3000K. Side light lifts texture, softens shadows on faces (you, your guests), and makes the act of coming home feel like a welcome rather than an inspection.

Round water-hyacinth basketShop on Amazon

There are two correct configurations. The first is a pair of plug-in linen-shade sconces flanking a mirror or framed print above the console — symmetrical, soft, and unmistakably composed. The second is a single small table lamp on the console itself, usually with a woven or paper shade, throwing a pool of light onto the wall behind it. Either reads as deliberate; both look terrible with an overhead fixture also blazing. If you have a hardwired ceiling light, put it on a separate switch and use it only when you're cleaning.

Bulb choice is non-negotiable: 2700K colour temperature, 60 to 75 lumens per watt equivalent, dimmable if possible. A smart bulb set to "warm" at 30% brightness, paired with a motion sensor by the door, is the quiet luxury upgrade — you walk in, the light fades up, you've never touched a switch. The lighting we use A pair of CADUKE plug-in swing-arm sconces with linen shades flanking the mirror, or a single JONATHAN Y hyacinth basket table lamp on the console. Either reads as deliberate — and both pair with a warm 2700K bulb, dimmable, never above 3000K.

4. One calm vignette — the rule of three

Three things on the console. A lamp, a small tray for keys and post, and one quiet object — a stoneware bowl, a single stem in a small vase, a stack of two books with a ring dish on top. That's it. The empty space around them is the point: it's what separates a calm Japandi entry from a cluttered American one, where the catch-all becomes a stress monument by Wednesday.

This is the same odd-number rule that governs every Japandi surface — odd numbers feel composed because the eye can't pair them off symmetrically. The tray holds the small stuff that would otherwise spread across the surface (mail, AirPods, change), and gets emptied weekly. The single stem changes with the seasons: pampas in autumn, eucalyptus in winter, dried wheat in summer. One muted accent — a single terracotta-toned object, a small block of charcoal black — is plenty. More than that and the composition starts to chatter.

The tray we use A 17" acacia-wood tray with cut-out handles — warm honey grain that ages well, deep enough to contain a week's worth of mail and keys, slim enough not to dominate the console.

5. The mirror or the print — scale and height

Above the console you need one focal point, and the choice between a mirror and a framed print is functional. A mirror does three things at once: it reflects the warm sconce light back into the room, it doubles the apparent depth of a small entry, and it gives you a last-glance check before you leave the house. A framed print — a single sumi-e ink piece, a botanical, a quiet landscape — does none of that, but reads as more considered and is the right call in a wider entry where you don't need the depth trick.

Scale is the most-missed rule in the entire room. The mirror or frame should span roughly 60 to 75% of the console's width — postage-stamp art floating above a substantial console is the dead giveaway of a rented-feeling space. Height matters too: the bottom edge of the mirror or frame sits 8 to 12 inches above the console surface, which puts the eye-level of an average adult in the centre of the reflection or composition. Too high and it disconnects from the console below; too low and it crowds the lamp.

Round mirrors soften a rectangular console; rectangular mirrors mirror the lines of the console and read as more formal. There's no right answer — the round mirror tends to feel more residential and the rectangle more architectural. Either way, frame material matches one element below: a black-framed mirror picks up the cast-iron hooks; a natural rattan mirror echoes the basket. 6. The threshold rug — where outside meets in

The rug is the move most people skip, and it's the one that finishes the room. A jute or wool runner sized to the console — a few inches longer than the console at each end, stopping short of the door swing — grounds the whole composition and creates the subtle transition from outside to in. Without it, the entry feels half-furnished, like the console is hovering in a hallway.

Size first: in a narrow entry, a 2'6" by 6' or 2'6" by 8' runner is the standard. In a square entry where you genuinely have room, a 4' by 6' rug works and gives the eye somewhere to land. Bare floor at the threshold reads as cold and unfinished; an oversized welcome mat that crowds the door reads as rented. The Japandi sweet spot is roughly two thirds the console width, ending six inches before the door swing.

Material is climate-driven. Jute is the default for a dry, mild climate — durable, naturally warm-toned, hides the inevitable dirt. In wet climates, a flat-weave wool or a low-pile wool-jute blend handles damp boots better and is easier to spot-clean. Avoid anything synthetic with a rubber backing; it traps moisture and reads as cheap. Find runners and entry rugs in the textiles edit.

Five entryway mistakes that flatten the whole feeling

Each of these is the inverse of one of the moves above — the easiest way to learn the rules is to see them broken.

  1. The console that's too deep. Anything over 14 inches in a narrow hall narrows the path and turns the entry into an obstacle course. Even a beautiful console fails this test if it's the wrong depth — measure twice.
  2. Overhead light only. An overhead fixture, even at warm temperature, flattens every surface and gives the entry a flat, evaluative quality. Side light from a pair of sconces or a single table lamp is non-negotiable.
  3. The catch-all surface. Keys, mail, charger, a bowl of receipts, a hat, the dog's leash. By Wednesday the console is a stress monument. A tray contains the small stuff; everything else goes in the lidded box or the basket.
  4. Mismatched scale on the art or mirror. A postage-stamp print floating above a substantial console is the most-common entryway tell. Two thirds of the console's width, bottom edge 8 to 12 inches above the surface — those two numbers fix almost every wall-above-console mistake.
  5. No rug, or the wrong rug. Bare floor at the threshold feels cold and provisional; an oversized welcome mat reads as rented. A correctly-sized jute or wool runner is the move that lifts the entry from "functional" to "finished".

Common questions

How deep should an entryway console be?
Aim for 10 to 12 inches deep in a tight hall, 14 to 16 inches if the space is more generous. Anything deeper turns the console into an obstacle. Depth is the single number that decides whether the entry feels calm or crowded.

What height should a mirror or print be above the console?
Hang the bottom edge 8 to 12 inches above the console surface, with the artwork itself spanning roughly two thirds of the console's width.

Can you skip the console if the entryway is tiny?
Yes. A wall-mounted floating shelf at chest height does the same work in half the depth — a place to land keys, a small lamp, one quiet object.

Why no overhead light?
Overheads flatten everything and create a hospital-arrival feel. Side light from sconces or a single table lamp at warm 2700K lifts texture and softens shadows.

How many objects belong on the console?
Three. A lamp, a small tray, and one quiet object. The empty space around them is the point.
