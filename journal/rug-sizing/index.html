Why most rugs are too small (and how to size yours)

Walk into any badly-decorated living room and there's a 5x7 rug centered under the coffee table, with the sofa floating two feet behind it. The rug is too small. This is the single most common styling mistake in North American homes, and it's also the easiest to fix once you know the right sizes.

The rule for living rooms

The front legs of every major piece of seating should sit on the rug. All four legs is better. None of the legs is the mistake.

What that means in practice: in a standard living room with a sofa and two chairs, the rug is at least 8x10. If it's a bigger room, 9x12. A 5x7 rug doesn't fit any real living room — it's the dimensions for a foyer or a bathroom. Ignore the showroom photos that show small rugs floating between furniture; they look wrong in person.

The rule comes from how the eye reads a room. A rug visually anchors the seating arrangement — without it, the furniture floats. With a too-small rug, the furniture floats AND the rug looks lonely. With a properly sized rug, the seating becomes a defined area and the room feels composed.

The rule for dining rooms

The rug extends 24 inches past the table edge in every direction. So a 6-foot dining table needs a rug that's at least 10x8 — the table is 6x3, plus 4 feet of rug around it (24 inches per side).

Why 24 inches? Because that's the room a chair needs to slide back from the table without going off the rug. A chair half-on, half-off the rug is the dining room version of the same floating problem. It snags, it tips, it looks unfinished.

For round dining tables, use a round rug. The rug's diameter = table diameter + 48 inches. A 4-foot round table = an 8-foot round rug.

The rule for bedrooms

Three options, all valid:

  • Under the bed entirely. The rug extends past the foot of the bed by 24 inches and past each side by 18-24 inches. For a queen bed, that means an 8x10 rug. King = 9x12.
  • Under the bottom two-thirds of the bed. The rug starts under the bed at the foot of the nightstands and extends past the foot of the bed by 36 inches. Smaller rug works (6x9 for queen). Cheaper, harder to align.
  • Two small rugs at the bedside. Two 2x6 runners on each side of the bed. Works in small bedrooms where a big rug doesn't fit. Less visually anchored but more flexible.

Our default recommendation: option 1, the full rug under the bed. It's the most styled and the most cohesive.

The material order

For Japandi, the material hierarchy is jute → wool → cotton, in that priority. Jute is the workhorse — it's what we use under most living rooms. Wool is for bedrooms (softer underfoot, warmer). Cotton flatweave is for kitchens and bathrooms (washable). All three are on our Textiles pillar.

The verified jute pick: KEMA Jute Braided 8x10 (4.5★/228 reviews). Strong base for any living room.

The simple test

Stand in the doorway of the room. Look at the rug. If you can see floor between the rug edge and any major piece of furniture, the rug is too small. That's it. Three seconds, every time.
