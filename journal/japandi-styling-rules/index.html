Japandi styling rules

You can own all the right pieces and still get a room wrong. Styling is the difference between "nice furniture" and a room that actually feels calm. These are the rules that do it.

Once you've bought well (the right materials and palette), styling is what's left — and it's mostly about restraint. Six rules carry almost every Japandi room.

1. Leave it half-empty

The first rule is to stop adding. Empty space is a material; a surface that's mostly bare lets the few things on it matter. This is the single biggest shift most rooms need — the whole case is in the art of negative space.

2. One stem, not a bouquet

A single dried branch in a stoneware vase says more than a full arrangement. One considered object beats three competing ones — the single-stem rule applies to almost everything you place.

3. Odd numbers, varied heights

When you do group objects, use three (or one), never two or four, and vary the heights — tall, medium, low — so the eye moves. A trio of a vase, a small stack of books, and a low bowl is the classic vignette.

4. Balance the weight

Spread the visual weight around the room instead of loading one side. A rough guide is the 30-30-30 rule — split attention between the big pieces, the mid-layer, and the small accents so nothing clumps.

5. Style the surfaces

Nightstands, consoles, and shelves are where rooms are won or lost up close. Keep each to a few objects with a job — a lamp, a book, one ceramic. The five-object rule turns any surface into a vignette.

6. Warmth without clutter

The hardest balance: cosy but not busy. You get there with texture and light, not more objects — a throw, a warm bulb, a worn wood surface. More on warmth without clutter and layering textiles.
