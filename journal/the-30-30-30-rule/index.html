The 30-30-30 wall rule for Japandi rooms

Most rooms get walls wrong in two opposite directions. Either there's too much on them — gallery walls of fifteen frames, art stacked above the sofa, three pieces above the bed — or there's nothing at all, just a flat empty plane. The fix is the 30-30-30 rule: thirty percent art, thirty percent texture, forty percent empty. Once you can see those proportions, every wall in the house gets easier.

What the proportions mean

Stand in front of any wall and divide it visually into ten parts. Three of those parts should be "art" — framed prints, sculptural objects, anything with intentional visual weight. Three parts should be "texture" — not art, but not empty either: a wood mirror, a woven basket on a shelf, a fabric panel, a hung throw. The remaining four parts should be empty — visible cream paint, no objects, no frames, breath.

This is the proportion that reads "calm" instead of "decorated" or "sparse." It's a Japandi-specific rule, drawn from how Japanese tea rooms balance wall art (the alcove scroll) against negative space (the rest of the wall).

The art third

The Japandi default for wall art is one larger piece, not many smaller ones. A single 24x36 framed print or one large photograph reads more calm than six 8x10 frames. The reason is the same as the single-stem rule: one focal point gives the eye somewhere to land.

If you must hang a gallery wall (the family-photo wall, the kid's-art wall), keep it tight: 4-5 frames, asymmetric arrangement, all in the same wood-tone or all in matte black, with at least 4 inches of space between each frame. The gallery wall is the exception, not the rule.

The texture third

Texture on a wall is what keeps it from feeling sterile without filling it with art. Five reliable wall textures, in order of impact:

  • A round oak-framed mirror. Adds wood texture, reflects light from the rest of the room, doubles the visible space. The single most useful wall object after art. A 24-30 inch round mirror anchors any wall.
  • A linen-fronted floor mirror. Same idea, vertical. Adds wood + the natural texture of the linen face.
  • A small floating shelf with three objects. Wood shelf (oak), three objects in different heights (a small framed piece, a ceramic, a single book). Reads as a curated vignette without being "art."
  • A hung throw or fabric panel. A wool blanket on a wood rod, or a linen panel pinned at the top. Adds texture in the most literal sense — actual fabric on the wall.
  • A pendant light hanging from the wall corner. Less common but striking. The pendant becomes part of the wall composition, adding texture (the rattan or paper) and warmth (the bulb).

The empty 40 percent

This is the part most rooms get wrong. The empty section of the wall is intentional — it's what gives the art and the texture room to breathe. If you're tempted to fill it (with another frame, a vase on a shelf, a clock), the rule has been broken.

The empty section is also where the lighting matters most. A warm sconce on an empty wall casts shadow patterns that change through the day. A pendant lamp's shadow, an oblique afternoon shaft of sun — these become the wall's "art" without you needing to add anything.

Room-specific applications

Behind the bed: one large piece centered above the headboard (the art third), nothing else on the wall (the empty 40 percent), and a small wood-framed piece on each nightstand surface to flank (the texture third, applied at desk height instead of wall height).

Above the sofa: one large photograph or print, centered, with at least 8 inches of space above the sofa back. Don't place a row of three small pieces above a long sofa — it almost always reads off-balance.

Hallway walls: The Japandi hallway is the most "empty 40 percent" wall in the house. One framed piece, alone, halfway down the hall. The rest of the wall stays empty. The hallway becomes a place where you can breathe between rooms.

The squint test

Stand in the doorway and squint at the wall. If you see one or two distinct visual moments separated by a clear empty area, you're in the rule. If you see a busy collage of shapes, there's too much. If you see a flat empty plane, there's not enough.

The squint test takes three seconds and replaces hours of "what should I hang here?" deliberation. Squint, decide, move on.
