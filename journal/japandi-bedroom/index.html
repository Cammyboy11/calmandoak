The Japandi bedroom

If you're only going to make one room Japandi, make it this one. The bedroom already wants to be quiet — you're mostly removing, not adding. Here's the whole playbook.

Why this guide: the bedroom is the room I always recommend starting with — it's the room where Japandi works fastest, and it was the first room I rebuilt when we moved into the home Calm & Oak grew out of. Every product I name below is in our own bedroom. More about Calm & Oak →

The bedroom is the easiest room to make Japandi because it already runs on the five principles — calm, low, natural, restrained. The bedroom doesn't want a TV or work surfaces; it doesn't want guests; it doesn't want a centrepiece. It wants to disappear so you can sleep. Japandi just makes that explicit.

That means the work here is mostly removal, not addition. You're not buying ten new things; you're swapping three (the bed height, the bedding, the lighting) and stopping. Most people are 70% of the way to a Japandi bedroom after a single weekend of effort, which is why we always recommend starting here.

1. Lower the bed

Japandi beds sit low — eight to twelve inches off the floor — on visible legs or a simple slatted platform. A low bed does three things at once: the room feels taller because there's more visible air above the mattress; the room feels wider because the bed has less visual weight; and the room feels grounded because the eye reads the bed as part of the floor rather than as a separate piece of furniture squatting in the middle.

The single biggest move you can make in any bedroom is to swap a tall divan or storage-base frame for a low oak or oak-tone platform. It changes the proportions of the whole room before you've changed anything else.

If replacing the frame isn't realistic, the next-best move is a slim oat-linen upholstered headboard that quietens the existing setup. Keep the headboard short (under 36 inches) and unbuttoned — high padded headboards fight the calm by becoming the room's centrepiece.

What to avoid: storage drawers under the mattress (they push the bed higher and read as bulky); ornate carved frames; brass or chrome bed frames; and any "platform" bed with built-in nightstands attached — they read as a single tall unit instead of a low bed with separate bedside pieces.

2. Switch to washed linen

Bedding is where Japandi is won or lost. Stonewashed linen is the foundation: it looks better rumpled than cotton looks pressed, it breathes in every season, and it softens for years if you treat it right. (Read how to care for linen — the short version: don't iron, don't bleach, gentle detergent only. The pieces last decades.)

Pick one tonal family and commit to it. Three colours to choose from:

  • Oat — the warmest neutral, plays with light oak, almost universally flattering.
  • Sand — slightly deeper than oat, reads more grounded, pairs with walnut accents.
  • Sage — the muted-accent option (this becomes your one moment of colour for the whole room).

Layer in three weights: a fitted sheet (medium), a duvet in matching tonal family (heavy), and a folded throw at the foot (fine). The folded throw is the move that signals made bed without making the room look like a hotel room. 3. Make the bedside symmetrical

A matched pair of nightstands and a matched pair of lamps is what makes a bedroom read intentional instead of accidental. Symmetry is calming because the brain stops doing arithmetic — it gives up trying to balance the room and accepts that it's already balanced.

The matched pair is non-negotiable in a Japandi bedroom. The only acceptable alternative is one nightstand on one side and a matching floor lamp on the other — deliberately mismatched in a way that reads as a chosen composition, not a missing piece. What never works: two different nightstands at different heights, a lamp on one side and a stack of books on the other, or worse, a charger and a phone parked on the floor in lieu of a surface.

On each nightstand, the five-object rule applies: a lamp, a small dish or ring tray, a book, a water carafe or candle, and a small piece of art or single stem. Anything more becomes visual noise; anything less reads as unfinished. Put the lamps on warm 2700K bulbs and dimmers — see bedside lamp pairs in the lighting edit.

4. Warm the light, quiet the palette

Overhead light has no place in a Japandi bedroom after dark. The whole formula is two bedside lamps in 2700K, maybe a paper floor lamp in the corner, and a dimmer for everything. That's it. The reason is biological as much as aesthetic: warm low light tells the body it's evening, which is what a bedroom is for. (Why warm layered light matters in every room, not just the bedroom.)

The palette runs in parallel. Pick one warm-neutral base (oat, cream, soft sand) and one wood family (light oak as primary, walnut as accent). Then add one muted moment — sage on a cushion, terracotta in a single ceramic, charcoal in a framed print. Never two. The palette guide has five combinations that always work.

The decoration test: a Japandi bedroom needs no more than one dried stem in a stoneware vase on the dresser and one framed piece on the wall opposite the bed (placed so it's the first thing you see in the morning, not above the headboard where you never look). Anything beyond that — gallery walls, plants on every surface, multiple framed prints — is a different aesthetic.

What Japandi bedrooms get wrong — the five mistakes

Each mistake is the inverse of a section above. The bedroom is the easiest Japandi room to land — but these five trip up most people anyway.

  1. The high bed. By far the most common mistake. Instinct says "frame the bed up to make it feel substantial"; Japandi says lower the bed so the room reads taller. The frame is the single biggest swap.
  2. Crisp pressed cotton bedding. Hotel-bedroom cotton ironed flat reads as a guest room, not a bedroom you live in. Stonewashed linen, rumpled, is the move — and it gets better with every wash.
  3. Mismatched bedside. One nightstand, no nightstand, a stool on one side and a "real" nightstand on the other, two different lamps at different heights. The bedroom registers as incomplete even when the rest of the room is right.
  4. Overhead light only. Bright top-down ceiling light kills the calm. Two bedside lamps on dimmers, both 2700K, and the overhead fixture off after sunset — every time.
  5. Accent overload. A sage cushion and a terracotta vase and a charcoal throw is three competing moments. Pick one place for the colour, leave the rest in the neutral family.

Japandi vs Scandinavian vs boho vs modern farmhouse bedrooms

The four most-Pinterest-saved bedroom aesthetics, side by side. The dividing decisions are usually the bed (height, frame, headboard), the bedding (linen vs cotton vs layered patterns), and the wall-and-light rules.

Japandi keeps the wood discipline of Scandinavian, removes the sheepskin, removes the pattern stacking of boho, and removes the painted shiplap of modern farmhouse — then adds a single framed sumi-e print and a single hand-thrown ceramic. The result is a bedroom that disappears so you can sleep.

Frequently asked

Can I keep my high bed and still go Japandi? Yes, but with discipline: thin frame, no padded headboard, simple linen bedding, lower-profile nightstands. A low platform bed is still the cleaner path — if you can swap it over a year or two, do.

What size rug do I need under a bed? Roughly two-thirds of the bed should sit on the rug, with the rug extending out past either side and the foot. For a queen, that's an 8×10 minimum; for a king, 9×12. A small rug at the foot reads as a placemat.

Can a Japandi bedroom be small? Yes — small bedrooms are easier. Less furniture, fewer rules to enforce. A low bed, one nightstand with a matching floor lamp on the other side, one framed piece, linen bedding, and a single oak shelf is a complete Japandi room in 100 square feet.

Do I need matching nightstands? Yes, for symmetry. The clean alternative is one nightstand and a matching floor lamp on the other side — never mismatched nightstands.

How is a Japandi bedroom different from a Scandinavian one? Scandinavian leans light and cosy — lots of white, pale wood, sheepskin, often a pastel accent. Japandi keeps the wood but warms the whites (plaster, oat, sand), removes the sheepskin, and adds wabi-sabi ceramics and a single muted accent. Scandinavian is cosy minimalism; Japandi is calm minimalism with patina.
