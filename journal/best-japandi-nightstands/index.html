The best Japandi nightstands

A bedside table is the smallest piece of furniture in the room and the one you touch most — the first thing your hand finds in the morning and the last surface you clear at night. A Japandi nightstand does that quiet job in an honest material: solid oak or walnut, cane set in a wood frame, a single drawer or an open shelf, low enough to reach lying down. Here are four bedside tables that get it right, from a budget rattan piece to a solid-wood stand, each verified and assessed honestly.

How we chose: every nightstand below was checked against the same bar — at least 4.3 stars, hundreds of real reviews, in stock and shipping now, and made from a genuine, honest material in a calm neutral. We held a hard line on materials: solid wood, cane and rattan in only. Several popular options were dropped on that line alone — laminate-over-particleboard cabinets dressed up with gold handles, a fluted "natural oak" stand that turned out to be engineered wood, and one well-reviewed round table that was particleboard under a printed walnut finish. What makes a nightstand "Japandi"

The same principle that governs the rest of the room applies in miniature at the bedside: the piece should be made from a natural, honest material, kept low and quiet in form, and finished in a calm neutral. A bedside table is small and close, touched every day, so the material matters more here than almost anywhere else in the house.

Material first. Solid oak, walnut and ash; cane and rattan set in a wood frame. These age well, feel solid to the hand, and show real grain rather than a printed pattern. The thing to avoid is the laminate cabinet — particleboard wrapped in a wood-effect foil — which chips at the corners, repeats its grain unnaturally, and feels hollow. It is the single most common way a "Japandi" nightstand goes wrong, and no amount of styling hides it for long.

Form stays low and quiet. A Japandi bedside table is a calm rectangle or a simple cylinder, not a fluted, ridged or chrome-legged statement. It should sit roughly level with the top of the mattress so you can reach it lying down. Skip the gold handles, the mirrored fronts and the built-in LED strips; the right piece earns attention through proportion and grain, not features.

Colour agrees with the room. Natural oak, warm walnut, the soft straw tone of cane. The nightstand should sit quietly within the bedroom's muted palette rather than introducing a new colour or a high-gloss sheen that pulls the eye in a room meant for rest.

One useful function, done well. A single drawer, an open shelf, or a drawer above a shelf — that is enough. The Japandi nightstand resists the urge to add charging stations, drink holders and three drawers of plastic runners. It gives you somewhere to put a book and a lamp, and gets out of the way.

Drawer, shelf or both

The one real decision is storage, and it comes down to how tidy you honestly are. A drawer hides the bedside clutter — the charger, the hand cream, the receipts — and keeps the surface clear, which is the forgiving choice for most people. An open shelf is lighter and more visually calm, and it looks beautiful holding a single folded throw or a stack of two books, but it stays serene only if you are disciplined about it.

The most flexible answer is a piece that offers both: a drawer to hide things and a shelf to show one or two. Three of the four picks below do exactly that, which is why they suit so many bedrooms — you decide, day to day, how much to tuck away. If you keep a famously messy bedside, lean toward the closed drawer; if your nightstand never holds more than a lamp and a book, the open shelf will reward you with the lighter look. Either way, the rule from our piece on styling a calm nightstand holds: the surface should carry three things, not ten.

One or a pair

A bedroom shared by two reads calmest with a matched pair, one each side of the bed, because symmetry is itself a kind of quiet. All four picks can be bought twice, and a pair of the same nightstand is the simplest way to make a bedroom feel considered rather than assembled piece by piece. If you sleep alone, or your bed sits against a wall on one side, a single nightstand is the honest choice — and it lets you spend a little more on one good piece, which is where the fully-assembled Bme Oliver earns its place.

Whichever you choose, keep the two sides loosely balanced rather than identical if you are not buying a true pair: similar height, similar wood tone, similar restraint. A solid-wood stand on one side and a wood-framed cane piece on the other can sit happily together, in the same way the two-woods rule lets a room hold more than one timber as long as the tones agree.

Common questions

What makes a nightstand Japandi?
A natural, honest material — solid oak, walnut, ash, cane or rattan — kept low and quiet in form and finished in a calm neutral, so it reads as a small piece of furniture rather than a storage box. Skip laminate, gloss, gold handles and LED strips.

Drawer or open shelf?
A drawer hides the bedside clutter and keeps the surface clear, which suits most people. An open shelf is lighter and calmer but stays serene only if you are disciplined. A drawer above a shelf is the most flexible — somewhere to hide things, somewhere to show one or two.

What height should it be?
Roughly level with the top of the mattress, usually around 22 to 26 inches. Japandi beds sit low, so err toward the lower end if your bed is a low platform. The aim is a surface you can reach lying down.

Is cane or rattan durable enough?
Yes, for normal bedroom use — a nightstand is not a high-traffic piece. Keep woven panels out of prolonged direct sun and do not rest heavy objects on the weave. A cane accent set in a solid-wood frame is the lowest-maintenance way to get the look.

Are MDF or laminate nightstands ever okay?
They photograph well but rarely hold up to the honest-materials principle, and they give themselves away over time — chipped edges, repeating grain, a hollow feel. For a small, often-touched piece, solid wood or wood-framed cane is the better long-term buy.
