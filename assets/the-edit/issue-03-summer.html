Editor's noteIssue Three — Summer
The long light

Summer is about the space.

The almanac's name for high summer is taisho, the greater heat, and the kō inside it are some of the loveliest in the year: "warm winds blow," "lotus blossoms open," "earth is damp, air is humid." It is the only season the calendar describes through the air itself — the medium between things rather than the things.

That is summer's lesson for the home. The other seasons ask what to put in a room. Summer asks what to leave out of it, because heat and long light make a full room feel fuller, hotter, more. The cool room in July is not the one with more cooling objects. It is the one with more air — cleared surfaces, an open door, a single pale cloth where winter had three.

The Japanese have a word for exactly this: ma — 間 — the interval, the space between, the pause that makes the rest legible. And the Norwegians have the impulse the season turns on: friluftsliv, open-air living, the instinct to dissolve the line between inside and out. This issue is about both — the empty space, and the open door.

Open one door and leave it open. The room you want is the one with the garden in it.

Bleached & cool

Summer is the only season to let a little cool in — but coolness, not coldness. The trick is to keep the warm-neutral base and add one cool note: a stone-grey, a soft indigo on a single cushion, the bleached pale of oak left in the sun. Everything stays low and dry; the heat supplies all the intensity a room can hold.

The paletteIssue Three — Summer
Using the five

Cool, not cold

— Off-white on the walls bounces the long light without glaring. A true brilliant white turns blue and hard in July sun; keep the warmth in it.

— Oat is the cloth, lighter and less of it than in winter. One linen layer, not three. The bed loses its quilt; the sofa loses its throw.

— Bleached oak is the season's wood — pale, dry, the colour of driftwood and sun. It reads cooler than walnut without going grey.

— Cool stone is the new neutral object — a grey-glazed jug, an unglazed bowl. It is where the eye goes to feel a few degrees cooler.

— Faded indigo is the one cool accent, used as sparingly as winter's ember — a single dipped cushion, a stripe on a tea towel, the spine of one book. One note of blue in a warm room reads like shade.

Subtract a layer for the season. The coolest room in the house owns the least.

Shōsho — 小暑

Early July — "lesser heat," the gathering of summer before its peak. Its three kō are all motion and air.

First kō
Warm winds blow. The first evening you leave the door open and the inside and the outside become, briefly, one room.
Second kō
Lotus blossoms open. The flower the tradition prizes most — beautiful, and rooted in the mud it rises out of.
Third kō
Hawks learn to fly. The young of the year test the air. Everything is practising the long light while it lasts.
"The first warm night you leave the door open, the house grows a room."

A wordIssue Three — Summer

A word

Ma

Look at the character. A gate — 門 — with the sun showing through the gap in it. Ma is that gap: the interval, the pause, the space between. In Japanese thought it is not emptiness as lack but emptiness as substance — the silence that makes the music, the rest between two notes, the bare stretch of wall that lets the one print speak.

A Western eye sees an empty corner and wants to furnish it. The trained eye sees ma — a deliberate interval, doing work. In summer, when the heat tempts you to add fans, throws, cooling clutter, ma is the discipline that says: the open space is the cool. Leave it.

"The gap is not where the design stops. It is where the design happens."

The room

The room with the door open

The most summer thing a room can do is stop being only indoors. Built around a threshold — a door left open, a floor that runs out to a step — the room borrows the garden and gives back the cool of the house.

Calm & Oak08
The roomThe room with the door open

The Japanese house has always understood the threshold. The engawa — the wooden veranda that runs along the edge of a room, neither fully inside nor fully out — is the architecture of the in-between, a place to sit with the garden a step away and the shade behind you. Few of us have one. All of us have a door.

So the summer move is to treat one opening as the centre of the room. Pull the table or the two chairs toward it. Hang a sheer linen panel that moves in the draught and softens the glare without closing the view. Let the floor read continuously to the threshold — roll the heavy rug away and leave the bare boards running out to the light. The room is no longer a box with a window; it is a porch with a roof.

Then subtract for the heat. One pale cloth, not a layered nest. A jug of water and a single stem, not a tablescape. The cool of the room is its emptiness and its draught — the ma and the open door doing together what no fan does alone.

A second look

The bedroom, undressed

The summer bedroom is a subtraction too. Strip the bed back to one washed-linen layer — no quilt, no throw — in oat or the palest stone. Pull the heavy curtain and hang a sheer in its place so the early light comes in soft and you wake with it. Clear the nightstand to a single lamp, a glass of water, one book.

Linen is the whole technology of the season: it breathes, it wicks, it cools as cotton cannot, and it looks better creased than pressed — wabi in a fitted sheet. Buy it once, in a pale tone, and let it soften over years. It is the rare upgrade you feel every single night.

Material study

Why linen, every time

Linen is made from flax, one of the oldest cultivated fibres on earth, and almost nothing has improved on it for warm weather. The fibre is hollow, so it breathes and wicks; it conducts heat away from the body; and it relaxes into a soft, lived-in crease that cotton can only fake. Crucially for a calm home, it ages up — every wash makes it softer and more beautiful, the opposite of synthetics that age into landfill. Buy it pale, buy it once, and let the years do the rest.

"The only cloth that looks better the day after you forgot to iron it."

The editAn object
An object

The stoneware jug

Every season has its one object, and summer's is the water jug. A hand-thrown stoneware pitcher on the table is half utility and half ceremony: it keeps water cool through the porous clay, it makes the simple act of pouring feel considered, and it earns its place on a bare surface the way no plastic bottle ever could. Fill it in the morning, set it by the open door, and the table is dressed for the day.

Choose one with weight and a slightly irregular glaze — the mark of the hand, the small wabi imperfection that says it was made and not moulded. It will outlast every gadget you buy this summer, and it asks for nothing but water.

"Half utility, half ceremony — the most a single object can be."

The editA note on buying
A note on buying

Cool is mostly free

Almost everything that makes a room feel cooler costs nothing: the door left open, the heavy layer stored, the surface cleared, the rug rolled away. The few things worth buying are the ones you'll feel every day for years — linen you sleep on, a jug you pour from, a single low lamp for when the light finally goes.

So treat this edit as the short list it is. One linen upgrade, one good vessel, one warm evening light. The rest of summer's comfort you already own; you just have to take some of it away.

"The coolest thing you'll do for the room this season is open a door and close a cupboard."

The lessonIssue Three — Summer
The styling lesson

The space between

A room is not made only of its objects. It is made just as much of the intervals between them — the bare wall beside the print, the clear stretch of table, the gap on the shelf. Ma teaches that these are not absences waiting to be corrected. They are the design.

The test is simple and unforgiving: look at any surface and ask whether the empty parts feel intended or merely unfinished. An intended emptiness has edges — a margin held on purpose, an object given room to be seen. An accidental one feels like a job half done. The difference is entirely in the restraint: stopping one object sooner than the urge wants you to, and trusting the gap to carry the calm.

"Furnish the objects, then furnish the spaces between them. The second is the harder skill."

In practice

Three intervals to protect

Beside the art
Leave more bare wall around a single print than feels comfortable. The margin is what tells the eye the print matters. Crowd it and the print becomes wallpaper.
On the table
Keep one whole end of the table clear. Not for use — for air. A table with a held-open space reads calm even mid-meal.
In the doorway
Don't fill the threshold with a console or a plant. The open passage between in and out is the most valuable empty space the house has. Guard it.

The ritual

The long breakfast

Summer's gift is the early hour when the air is still cool and the light is already long. The slow-living move is to claim it — to make the first meal of the day a small, unhurried ceremony at the open door.

Calm & Oak22
The ritualThe long breakfast

There is no recipe, only a refusal to rush. Rise a little before you must. Carry the morning to the coolest, brightest threshold the house has — the open door, the step, the table pulled close to the light. Take it on a tray, so the carrying itself slows you down: a stoneware bowl, stone fruit, bread on a wood board, and something cold to drink.

The summer drink to learn is mugicha — Japanese roasted barley tea, caffeine-free, served cold. Steep a barley sachet in a jug of cold water overnight; by morning it is amber, nutty, faintly sweet, and pours over ice all day. It is the taste of a Japanese summer, it costs almost nothing, and a glass of it on a bare table is the whole season in a vessel.

Then sit, with the door open and the phone in another room, for as long as the morning allows. This is friluftsliv at its smallest and most achievable — not a hike, just a breakfast with the outside let in. Ten minutes of it changes a day.

The ritualThe in-between
The in-between

Make an engawa

You don't need a veranda to keep its idea. An engawa is simply a place to sit at the edge of inside — sheltered, but open to the air and the garden. Make one anywhere: a low bench at an open door, a cushion on a balcony threshold, a chair turned to a window thrown wide.

Its value is that it is neither one room nor the other. It is the ma between the house and the world — a few square feet that belong to the pause. Keep it clear, keep it simple, and let it be the place you go when the day asks too much. Some of the calmest minutes of a summer are spent exactly on that edge.

"The most valuable seat in a summer house is the one half-outside it."

This season

To read, to hear, to make

To read. White by Kenya Hara — the designer of Muji on emptiness, paper, and the Japanese sense that blank space is not nothing but a vessel for everything. A short, beautiful argument for the very ma this issue is built on.

To learn. The word shibui — the understated, the quietly excellent, the beauty that reveals itself slowly rather than at a glance. Summer rooms, at their best, are shibui: nothing showy, everything considered.

To make. Cold barley tea — mugicha — from page 23. One jug, steeped overnight, the whole summer in a glass.

To notice. The hour the light goes long and gold in the evening — your own golden hour. Mark where it falls in the room. Autumn's issue is built around it, and it is already arriving, a few minutes earlier each week.

"The blank space is not the absence of the design. It is the room the design needs to breathe."
